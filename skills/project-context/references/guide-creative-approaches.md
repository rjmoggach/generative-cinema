# Creative Approaches — Authorial Intent & Technique

Where the system stops being a rulebook and starts supporting *voice*. The other
guides give correct, legible craft; this one covers the choices that make work
expressive: why the camera moves, whose eyes we see through, what the image says
beneath the action, and when to break the rules on purpose. Use in
`project-context` (the creative brief) and `shot-prompt` (intent behind a shot).

Format: **Decision / Use when / Because / Prompt translation / Watch-outs / Anchors.**

---

## 1. Motivated camera — every move earns its reason

- **Use when:** deciding whether and how the camera moves.
- **Because:** a move with no narrative reason reads as restless or arbitrary.
  Movement should be motivated — by following action, revealing information, or by
  a character's internal emotional state. A push-in motivated by a realization
  *means* something; a drift for its own sake distracts.
- **Prompt translation:** tie the move to its cause in the prompt — "slow push-in
  as she realizes the truth", "handheld follow taking its energy from his panic".
- **Watch-outs:** static is a valid, strong choice; don't add motion by default.
  In AI video, an unmotivated move also invites artifacts — fewer, purposeful
  moves render cleaner.
- **Anchors:** motivated-camera principle; character-motivated cuts (Mackendrick).

---

## 2. Objective vs. subjective framing

- **Objective** — the camera observes from outside; the audience is a witness.
  Neutral angles, eye level, balanced. *Use for* clarity, irony, detachment.
- **Subjective** — the camera aligns with a character: their POV, their eyeline,
  their distortion. *Use for* identification, immersion, dread.
- **Because:** point of view decides *whose experience* the audience shares. A
  scene cut objectively feels reported; the same scene cut into a character's POV
  feels lived.
- **Prompt translation:**
  - POV shot — "first-person POV, what she sees, eyeline-matched height".
  - Subjective coloring — push lens/light toward the character's state (wide +
    low for threat, shallow + warm for longing).
- **Watch-outs:** match POV height/angle to the character (a child looks *up*);
  keep eyelines consistent per `guide-continuity-rules.md`.
- **Anchors:** subjective/objective POV; see `reference-visual-*.md`.

---

## 3. Visual subtext — what the image says that the dialogue doesn't

- **Use when:** the meaning of a beat differs from its surface (tension under
  politeness, distance between people who claim closeness).
- **Because:** blocking, lensing, and framing carry subtext the words withhold —
  who dominates the frame, who's isolated, what separates them, where the eye
  lingers. The audience reads feeling from staging, not just lines.
- **Prompt translation:** stage the subtext — "the two seated close but a vertical
  line of the doorframe splitting them", "he fills the frame, she's small and low
  in the corner", "she looks at him; he looks away".
- **Watch-outs:** let it be felt, not labeled; over-literal symbolism reads cheap.
- **Anchors:** staging for subtext; power geometry in dialogue (see film-grammar).

---

## 4. Motif & visual repetition

- **Use when:** you want resonance across a piece.
- **Because:** a repeated image, angle, color, or move accrues meaning — its
  return rhymes, and a variation on it signals change. This is how a film feels
  authored rather than assembled.
- **Prompt translation:** define a recurring motif in `project-context` (a
  framing, a color, a camera move) and deliberately restate or vary it at marked
  beats.

---

## 5. Intentional rule-breaking

The craft rules are defaults; breaking one *on purpose* is a tool. Break with a
reason and let it land on a story beat.

| Break | Effect it buys | Use when |
|---|---|---|
| Cross the 180° line | Disorientation, reversal, new "round" | A turning point or loss of control |
| Jump cut | Time lurch, anxiety, energy | Compress an activity; restless interior |
| Canted (Dutch) angle | Unease, wrongness | The world is off-balance |
| Flat/frontal symmetry | Artifice, control, irony | Formal worlds (Anderson, Kubrick) |
| Hold far too long | Discomfort, dread, contemplation | Force the audience to sit in it |
| Break eyeline / direct address | Rupture the fourth wall | Complicity, confrontation |

- **Because:** a broken rule is conspicuous; the audience feels the rupture. That
  salience is the point — spend it where the story turns.
- **Prompt translation:** name the break and the intent ("deliberate canted
  angle, world destabilized"); don't let it look like an accident.
- **Watch-outs:** break one thing at a time against an otherwise controlled frame,
  or it reads as incompetence rather than choice.

---

## 6. Genre as a starting grammar (not a cage)

Genres carry visual expectations — noir's low-key and verticals, horror's
negative space and wides, romance's warm shallow tele, doc's handheld available
light. Use them as a fast shared language with the audience, then subvert
deliberately for freshness. Anchor specific looks via `reference-film-movements.md`
and `reference-visual-*.md`.

---

## 7. Quick application

1. For each scene, state the **intent**: whose POV, what the subtext is, the feeling.
2. Choose **objective or subjective** framing to match.
3. **Motivate** any camera move; otherwise hold.
4. Plant or pay off a **motif** where it resonates.
5. Break a rule **only** with a reason, on a beat, one at a time.
